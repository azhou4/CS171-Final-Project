What is Social Mobility?



Social Mobility is the upward or downward movement of Americans from one social class or economic level to another, through job changes, inheritance, marriage, connections, tax changes, innovation, illegal activities, hard work, lobbying, luck, health changes or other factors.

A 2013 Brooking Institution study found income inequality was increasing and becoming more permanent, sharply reducing social mobility.

In particular, in recent years, several studies have found that intergenerational mobility -- the degree to which children do better or worse than their parents economically -- is lower in the US than in some European countries.

Social Mobility in the U.S. is Lower than in Other Countries'

The higher the intergenerational earning elasticity, the lower the chance of social mobility.

The United States has an intergenerational earning elasticity of 0.47, the 5th lowest out of these 17 countries.

Many Americans strongly believe the US to be a "land of opportunity" that offers every child an equal chance at social and economic mobility. The idea that Americans rise from humble origins to riches is part of the American Dream.

The American Dream Report, a study of the Economic Mobility Project, found that Americans surveyed were more likely than citizens of other countries to agree with statements like:

  • "People get rewarded for intelligence and skill"
  • "People get rewarded for their efforts"

and less likely to agree with statements like:

  • "Coming from a wealthy family is 'essential' and 'very important' to getting ahead"
  • "Income differences in my country are too large"
  • "It is the responsibility of government to reduce differences in income"

Does social mobility still exist in America?

As we've seen, social mobility depends on where you start and who you are. If you're a glass half full kind of person, you might think yes. After all, most people are better off than past generations were. Accounting for inflation, about 3 times as many Americans make incomes above $100,000 now than did in 1967. However, our data clearly shows that not all groups have benefitted equally from this economic growth. An individual's chance at upward mobility can vary greatly by race, gender, hometown, parental income, or other factors. There are still many inequalities in today's America that need to be addressed including racial segregation, the gender wage gap, and more. Manufacturing, an industry that historically provided stable jobs and decent pay to less-educated workers, has been declining for a while now and was particularly hard hit by the recession from 2007 to 2009. In the wake of this decline, most of the jobs available for less-educated workers tend to be low-paying service industry jobs, contributing to lower absolute mobility than we've seen in the past.

All this means is that the rungs of the social mobility ladder in the United States seem to be getting harder to climb.
